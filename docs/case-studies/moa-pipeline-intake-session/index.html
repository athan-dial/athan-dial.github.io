The Shadow Process Problem: Systematizing a Scientific Workflow

decision systems · team · medium

A biologist was running mechanism-of-action analysis through ChatGPT and Excel. The process worked. It was also unreproducible, unauditable, and about to break under a second collaboration. The fix started with an intake session, not a tool.

Context

A biology collaborator was doing mechanism-of-action analysis by pasting experimental data into ChatGPT sessions and manually curating outputs in Excel. She had real judgment about what mattered and what to filter. The process worked — for one person, on one dataset, with no audit trail.

A second collaboration was incoming. Different dataset, different scientists. The manual workflow had a clear ceiling, and we were about to hit it.

Ownership

I co-designed the replacement workflow with the biologist. My role: run a structured intake session, document what she was already doing precisely enough to formalize it, and build a pipeline that produced output she could react to.

Decision Frame

The easy path was to build a tool based on what we assumed the analysis needed. The right path was to understand the shadow process first.

When a scientist says “I paste data into ChatGPT,” what they usually mean is: I’m running a multi-step curation process in my head, using the chat as a workspace, and I’ve developed real judgment about what to keep and what to discard. That judgment is the thing you need to encode — not replace.

The intake session surfaced three inputs that weren’t obvious from the raw data alone:

  1. Phenotype description with specificity — including what the phenotype doesn’t match. The negative case constrains the target space.
  2. Pathway context with known regulators. The curated prior knowledge of which pathways matter, not the raw experimental file.
  3. Differentially expressed proteins as the evidence set linking phenotype to candidate mechanisms.

The raw file was the simpler default. What the session revealed was that the biologist’s judgment was already selecting and contextualizing before the analysis started — and that judgment needed to be made explicit and reproducible, not assumed away.

Scope was bounded intentionally: start with molecular mechanism of action, expand later. A testbed that produces something concrete to react to, not a complete system.

Outcome

A proof-of-concept pipeline shipped end-to-end. The output: a rank-ordered list of candidate targets with prescribed follow-up assays, published to a shared reporting environment for the biologist to review.

The design principle governing output format: scientists prescribe the follow-up assays. The pipeline surfaces candidates. Biology judgment decides what to test.

Reflection

The hard part of systematizing scientific workflows is the intake, not the technology.

Knowledge lives in a system or in someone’s head. This biologist’s analytical judgment lived entirely in her head — effective, but with a bus factor of one and no reproducibility. The intake session was how we moved it from implicit expertise to explicit, encodable process.

The three inputs we canonicalized came from that session. Not from a requirements document. Not from prior literature. From documenting what she was already doing, precisely enough to formalize it. That’s the discipline — understand the shadow process before you build anything.
