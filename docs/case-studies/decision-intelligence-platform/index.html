Decision Intelligence Needs Structure and Nerve

decision systems · organization · high

Rebuilding the decision capture layer—analytics, compliance, and publication architecture—so high-stakes compound selections traced back to verifiable data, not oral tradition.

On this page

  • Context
  • Ownership
  • Decision Frame

Context

Every compound selection round committed tens of thousands of dollars and months of lab time, yet six months later no one could answer “why these molecules?” The discovery programs generated assay hits, fail records, procurement events, and narrative briefs scattered across tools. None of it rolled up into a traceable decision log. Dashboards showed pass counts without fail counts, leaders asked for enrichment deltas we could not justify, and analyses vanished inside personal notebooks. The problem threatened to lock the next nomination cycle into high-dollar bets built on incomplete or biased data.

Ownership

I owned the data model design, compliance instrumentation, and publication lifecycle redesign end-to-end. I identified the structural gaps (missing fail entries, ephemeral decision rationale, undifferentiated wiki content), escalated the compliance risk to the Head of Research and compliance lead, and designed and shipped the three-layer stack. I also stress-tested the system by stepping away during a nomination crunch while my team executed against the new architecture—proving the system worked without me in the room.

Decision Frame

The core problem split into three failure modes: the funnel dataset was incomplete because several programs stopped entering fails after initial screening; decision rationale was ephemeral (lived in someone’s head or a system after the meeting ended); and the wiki-based publication platform had no lifecycle, so draft explorations and canonical learnings looked identical.

I faced a choice: keep shipping pretty dashboards with disclaimers, or re-architect the way the organization captured, audited, and published decisions. Without fixing the three failure modes, every applied AI claim about portfolio optimization was theater.

I chose re-architecture. I built a three-layer stack: a data model plus dashboards, compliance instrumentation, and a structured publication lifecycle.

Layer One: Data Model + Dashboards. I designed a program-level schema that tracked each target’s funnel counts, enrichment factors, procurement spend, and milestone status. The schema sat in Git, documented line-by-line, and fed a set of dashboards hosted on the internal publishing platform. Anyone—from medicinal chemists to the VP—could see the same view, but more importantly they could query the schema directly. The dashboards surfaced scenario comparisons for nominations, hit progression curves, and budget pacing, but the durable asset was the model with explicit definitions of “pass,” “fail,” and “nomination-ready.”

Layer Two: Compliance Instrumentation. During schema validation I noticed that certain programs had zero recorded fails after screening despite known downstream attrition. That meant our metrics overstated success and made enrichment analyses meaningless. Rather than bury a footnote, I quantified the gap (programs missing 100% of post-screening fails, stages lacking timestamps, assays without ownership tags) and escalated to leadership as a portfolio-risk issue: we were about to prioritize spend using biased metrics. I recommended a process fix (enforce fail entry, add ownership to each funnel stage, audit weekly) and offered instrumentation to monitor adherence. Escalation carried the known tradeoff of slowing teams down to record data, but the alternative was making high-dollar bets on fiction.

Layer Three: Publication Lifecycle. The wiki could not distinguish between an idea, an analysis, and a canonical conclusion. I codified a lifecycle—Question → Learning Agenda → Analysis → Brief → Canon—and embedded it in the internal publication platform. Each stage had gates: a Question documented the open problem; a Learning Agenda scoped methods, datasets, and collaborators; an Analysis produced reproducible results; a Brief translated findings for leadership; Canon archived the sanctioned answer with links to data, code, and decisions. I shipped templates, metadata requirements, and review workflows so analysts knew what “done” meant. Dashboards linked back to Canon entries, closing the loop between visualization and written justification. I also seeded the platform with two operational reporting frameworks describing how we measure decision quality (completeness, timeliness, outcome capture) and analytical throughput (questions answered, analyses promoted to canon).

Outcome

Several things changed immediately. The documented schema became an organizational contract; teams referenced it in cross-functional reviews, and engineers used it to validate ingestion jobs. Dashboards now exposed completeness metrics next to funnel charts, so missing fails rendered as red bars rather than hidden footnotes. After the escalation, research leadership instituted a weekly audit of fail entries and tied compliance to program reviews, closing the structural gap.

Analytical work stopped disappearing. Questions flowed into Learning Agendas, and analyses either graduated to Briefs or were archived with rationale. When leadership asked “what do we know about AI-prioritized enrichment at stage two?”, there was a Canon entry citing the data, assumptions, and decision log instead of a scramble for old slides.

The publication platform v2 elevated reporting. Analysts published compound investment briefs that linked to dashboards, referenced compliance status, and captured decisions verbatim. Those briefs became onboarding material for new scientists, reducing the time to context. The operational reporting frameworks gave us a way to talk about our own performance: how many questions entered the pipeline, how many analyses advanced, how quickly findings made it to Canon. That measurement discouraged sandbagging because throughput was visible.

The clearest proof of leverage came during a nomination crunch I mentioned: the team delivered a nomination package without me, leadership approved it, and procurement executed—all because the process was encoded in system artifacts, not oral tradition. Decision intelligence went from “ask the analyst” to “inspect the stack.”

Reflection

Decision intelligence only works when analytics, compliance, and publication move in lockstep; otherwise you automate pretty dashboards over biased data and call it insight. The key insight was treating the data model as the source of truth first, and dashboards as a view into it—not the other way around. That distinction kept the organization from optimizing for visualization at the cost of correctness.

The escalation of the fail-entry gap was essential. Pushing back on teams’ data entry habits created friction, but it prevented the organization from making portfolio decisions based on systematically biased data. The tradeoff was explicit and owned.

Finally, encoding the publication lifecycle in platform features and templates turned “good analytical practice” from an aspiration into a repeatable process. It’s easier to follow a structured workflow when the system enforces gates, and visibility into throughput creates the right incentives for teams to move work forward rather than let it sit.
