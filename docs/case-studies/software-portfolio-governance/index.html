100+ Internal Tools and No Way to Know Which Ones Matter

decision systems · organization · medium

Over 100 internal software tools sat in a catalog tagged 'active' or 'inactive.' That binary told leadership nothing about which ones would cause a crisis if they broke. I designed a two-axis risk model to make the exposure visible.

Context

Over 100 internal software tools were imported into an application catalog. The catalog had a comprehensive list of what existed. It didn’t have a useful answer to the harder question: which of these, if they stopped working tomorrow, would cause a real problem?

Each tool was tagged with a lifecycle status — active or inactive. A leadership presentation was due in four days. I had until then to turn a flat list of apps into something that could actually guide governance decisions.

The problem with a single status field is what it conceals. “Active” doesn’t tell you whether a tool handles mission-critical workflows or runs on a test server someone forgot to shut down. It doesn’t tell you whether an engineer owns it or whether it’s maintained by tribal knowledge and hope. Active just means it’s running.

Ownership

I was the Product Lead for the application catalog — responsible for the category framework, the status taxonomy, and the governance architecture.

The catalog had been inherited in an incomplete state: two fields existed (business criticality and lifecycle status), but the mismatch between them was invisible. A high-criticality tool could be in a poor support state and the catalog wouldn’t surface it. The risk was present but unobserved.

I owned the two-axis risk model, the four-tier classification system, the schema alignment between the business catalog and the engineering metadata, and the leadership presentation that translated the architecture into a narrative.

Four days, no full audit, real deadline.

Decision Frame

The core design question: what makes a software governance framework actually useful? The answer I arrived at: it makes risk visible in a form that’s actionable. Not “here’s a comprehensive list” — but “here’s specifically where you’re exposed.”

Adding the missing axis

The catalog already had business criticality (how much does the org depend on this?) and lifecycle status (is it running?). The gap was support level — how much engineering backing does it actually have?

  • Production — an engineer owns it, incidents get triaged, it’s on the uptime list
  • Supported — someone will eventually fix it if it breaks
  • Best-effort — if this breaks during a critical decision window, there is no recovery path

That distinction between “supported” and “best-effort” is the line that changes the governance question. Without it, “active + high criticality” looks fine. With it, “active + high criticality + best-effort support” is a liability.

Four operational tiers

The combination of business criticality and support level surfaces four meaningful categories:

  • T1 — Production-Critical: High criticality, production-level support. These are what a crisis response protects first.
  • T2 — Load-Bearing, Under-Supported: High criticality, best-effort backing. These are the exposure — they matter enough to cause problems but aren’t backed enough to guarantee recovery.
  • T3 — Supported, Non-Critical: Lower criticality, reasonable support. Standard maintenance posture.
  • T4 — Experimental: Low criticality, low support. Where things are being tried.

T2 is the interesting tier. T2 tools aren’t failures. They’re tools that grew to be load-bearing faster than engineering backing caught up. That’s a normal condition in any organization that moves fast. The question isn’t whether T2 exists — it does — but whether you can see it. The tradeoff becomes obvious instead of theoretical once you can filter the catalog by tier and show leadership exactly where the exposure sits.

Schema alignment

The catalog had a structural conflict: my business taxonomy needed to coexist with the engineering metadata schema used by the development team. Two systems, potentially two different classifications of the same tools.

Three options: force alignment (high friction, breaks existing tooling), accept duplication (inconsistencies accumulate), or treat them as complementary layers with a clear division of ownership and a minimal sync surface.

I chose the two-layer approach. The catalog owns the business layer: criticality, support level, tier, owner. The engineering schema owns the technical layer: dependencies, build status, deployment config. The two systems link where they need to, but neither forces the other to replicate its logic.

The guiding constraint: if you have to enter the same field in two places, you’ve created a divergence waiting to happen.

Outcome

The tier model was adopted. The support level field was added to the catalog. T2 apps and policy violations surfaced as a filtered view — a concrete risk register for the first time.

The leadership presentation was delivered on schedule, leading with the headline question and working through the two-axis surface, tier definitions, and T2 register as the actionable output.

The two-layer schema design was adopted — engineering metadata and business catalog now operate as complementary layers.

What I don’t yet have: comprehensive T2 attribution across the full portfolio. The tier model is in place; the full catalog audit is ongoing. The architecture is built; the data population continues.

Reflection

The governance problem — 100+ apps, four days, leadership presentation — was a simpler problem underneath: how do you make risk observable in a catalog?

The answer wasn’t a better list. It was a better question. “What do we have?” produces a catalog. “Where is our risk?” produces a risk register. The second question requires different data — specifically, the mismatch between how much something matters and how well it’s supported.

Vocabulary precision compounds over time. Distinguishing “retired” from “decommissioned” as separate lifecycle statuses is a small distinction. But in a framework used by multiple teams, small distinctions either accumulate as clarity or decay into confusion. Spending five minutes getting terminology right pays off every time someone reads the catalog and correctly understands what they’re looking at.

Governance frameworks that feel like audit findings get filed away. Ones that feel like operational intelligence get used. The framing is the difference — and getting it right is as much a part of the design work as the schema.
