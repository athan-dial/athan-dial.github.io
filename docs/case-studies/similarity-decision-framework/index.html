Similarity Decisions Need Weighted Frameworks

decision systems · organization · high

How a weighted decision matrix converted a contentious technology debate into a transparent artifact, turning opinions into evidence and preserving architectural flexibility.

On this page

  • Context
  • Ownership
  • Decision Frame
  • Outcome
  • Reflection

Context

Chemists needed similarity results under 60 seconds to support a 22-requirement PRD and an immovable Phase 0 milestone. The backend debate was drifting toward vendor demos and gut feel. Choosing between Postgres+RDKit or Milvus on Kubernetes without structure risked burning a sprint while still producing an argument with no clear resolution.

Ownership

I owned the decision architecture and stakeholder alignment. I built the weighted framework, walked chemists and platform engineers through the rationale, and designed the API abstraction that would preserve optionality regardless of which backend won.

Decision Frame

The Problem: Postgres already ran inside our platform team with ingestion pipes flowing from the warehouse. RDKit gave chemists substructure plus similarity in plain SQL. Milvus, stewarded by a two-person modeling group, offered billion-scale approximate nearest neighbor search but required new ingestion, new ops, and a separate chemistry layer. Everyone optimized a different dimension depending on their vantage point.

The Real Decision: Not “which engine benchmarks faster?” but “which dimensions matter most right now, given our product commitments and staffing model?” Without shared weights we risked selecting the shiniest technology, missing Phase 0, and being unable to explain the choice to leadership later.

My Approach: I defined five weighted dimensions tied to our constraints:

  • Product Fit & Workflow (25%) — RDKit cartridges natively expose substructure search, multiple fingerprint families, and scaffold analysis through SQL. Milvus would require an RDKit sidecar to regain chemistry awareness.
  • Data Alignment (20%) — Our dbt models already write compound tables into Postgres nightly. Milvus had no ingestion path and would introduce another ETL layer.
  • Scalability & Performance Ceiling (20%) — Milvus’s strength: embedding-native ANN queries and clean separation from OLAP workloads.
  • Ownership/Ops & Staffing (20%) — Postgres lived inside the platform team’s on-call rotation. Milvus would live on a modeling-managed Kubernetes namespace with no SLOs.
  • Delivery Risk for Phase 0/1 (15%) — Postgres already covered six PRD requirements and passed early integration tests. Milvus was still a proof of concept.

I wrote the output at three fidelities: the one-liner for Slack, the one-pager for Tech Ops with the table and weights, and a three-pager detailing 30+ comparative points, ingestion flow diagrams, staffing matrices, and the proposed Similarity Service API. That API abstracted both engines as derived indexes so the UI and business logic never touched a specific backend.

Because decisions stick when people understand them, I walked stakeholders through the weights. Chemists agreed product fit deserved the highest weight because they wanted substructure search to feel like tools they already used. Platform engineers agreed ownership mattered because they were on the hook for uptime. Modeling leads agreed scalability mattered but acknowledged we weren’t at billion-compound scale yet. By the time the Tech Ops meeting happened, disagreement had shifted from “Postgres vs. Milvus” to “should scalability be 20% or 30%?"—a far healthier conversation.

Outcome

Postgres won four of five weighted dimensions; Milvus won scalability. Leadership approved the recommendation four days after the memo circulated. Engineers shipped the Phase 0 API backed by Postgres, giving chemists their 60-second workflow on schedule.

The Milvus proof of concept remained in source control with instructions for running side-by-side comparisons, preserving a future upgrade path without burning time now. Documentation from the three-pager became part of the similarity search Canon entry on our publication platform, so months later when someone asked “why not Milvus?” we pointed to the decision table instead of relitigating the whole stack.

The Similarity Service abstraction already expects multiple backends, so when we pilot Milvus for research-scale embedding search we’ll do it behind the same interface without ripping up the UI or ingestion.

Most importantly, the weighted framework turned a contentious technology debate into a transparent decision artifact. People argued about weights and evidence, not about who could lobby hardest.

Reflection

Technology choices stay healthy when the decision architecture is explicit. Frameworks carry the leverage, not whichever database sounds more exciting this quarter. The key insight was reframing “which system is objectively better?” into “which dimensions matter most given our constraints?"—a question that admits shared ownership and leaves room for legitimate disagreement about priorities, not competence.
