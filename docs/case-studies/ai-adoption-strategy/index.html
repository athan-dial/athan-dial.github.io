AI Adoption Needs a Measurement Layer

decision systems · organization · high

Building a repeatable adoption system with onboarding, evidence, and iteration instead of one-time rollouts.

On this page

  • Context
  • Ownership
  • Decision Frame
  • Outcome
  • Reflection

Context

Leadership wanted the leverage of applied AI across the company, but uptake was uneven and unmeasured—strong in a few pockets and absent in others. If we let adoption spread ad hoc, we would pay for tools without learning where they helped, where they hurt, and what should be built next.

The problem was not access to AI tools; it was the absence of a repeatable adoption system. Some teams had integrated ChatGPT into daily workflows, others had not touched any AI tool, and there was no shared frame for what “good usage” looked like. Without an evaluation model, every decision defaulted to vibes: feature lists, vendor demos, and the loudest internal advocates. That produced a second-order risk: adoption would happen in pockets, but the organization would not get better at using these tools over time because the learning never accumulated.

Ownership

I designed and executed the end-to-end adoption strategy: built the custom workflow assistant, created the structured intake for automation ideas, and ran the dual-platform trial with instrumentation and preference measurement.

Decision Frame

The decision in front of me was whether to treat AI adoption as a one-time rollout (training session, policy doc, and a standard tool mandate) or to treat it like an internal product with onboarding, evidence, and iteration. A rollout optimizes for speed and compliance. An internal product optimizes for durable behavior change.

I chose the product approach, with the known tradeoff of investing time in instrumentation and user experience before “standardizing” on a single platform. The tradeoff becomes obvious instead of theoretical once you have real usage and preference data to work from.

I ran three moves over two cycles:

  1. Ship one company-wide workflow assistant to create a safe first touchpoint. In Sep 2025, during the annual OKR cycle, I built a custom GPT loaded with OKR best practices and context about the company’s strategic priorities. I rolled it out to all staff through our main Slack channel. The goal was not to “teach AI.” The goal was to give every colleague a low-friction reason to try an AI tool on a task they were already doing, inside the tools they already used. That reduced coordination overhead and made experimentation safe because the output was a draft, not a production artifact. The known tradeoff was that goal-setting is not the highest-leverage place to apply AI. I chose it anyway because it created a first shared experience and revealed onboarding friction fast.

  2. Create a structured intake for automation ideas so the organization could express demand. In Jan 2026, after an internal hack day where several teams built AI-powered prototypes, I created a structured Confluence page where anyone could propose team-facing AI automation ideas. This was a demand-capture mechanism: it surfaced workflow pain points in the language of the teams doing the work, and it made the backlog legible to leadership and builders. It also created a governance line: ideas were captured in one place, with enough context to be evaluated, rather than being re-litigated in Slack threads. The known tradeoff was that an open intake can collect more ideas than any team can build, which can disappoint contributors. I mitigated that by designing the page so proposals were specific and evaluable—framed around “what work would change” rather than “what model would we use.”

  3. Run a side-by-side platform trial with usage tracking and a preference survey so leadership could decide based on measured behavior. In Apr 2026, I ran a company-wide side-by-side evaluation of two leading AI platforms. Instead of choosing a winner via feature comparison or vendor demos, I set up a trial where all users received access to both tools at the same time. I paired that access with two measurement mechanisms: usage tracking throughout the trial period and a preference survey at the end.

This was intentionally a product evaluation rather than a procurement exercise. The core questions were operational: which tool do people actually use, for what kinds of work, and what friction causes them to switch or stop? Login counts do not answer that. People can “try” a tool and then quietly abandon it. Usage patterns and stated reasons—captured through tracking and surveys—expose where the tool fits the work and where it breaks down. The known tradeoff was that a dual-platform trial introduces temporary complexity: users have two options, managers see inconsistent tool choices, and there is more surface area for support. I accepted that because the alternative is a forced single choice with little evidence, which creates hidden churn and shadow tooling.

Outcome

The custom GPT produced immediate adoption across teams that previously showed zero AI engagement, and it established a pattern the organization could repeat: start with a real workflow, ship something usable through the channels people already live in, and treat outputs as drafts that make people faster without raising risk.

The post-hackathon Confluence intake produced a pipeline of automation ideas grounded in real team needs, and several of those ideas moved into development. More importantly, it created a shared place where adoption demand could be expressed, evaluated, and prioritized without re-creating context every time the topic came up.

The dual-platform trial produced evidence leadership could use to make an investment decision based on measured user behavior and stated preferences across the organization, rather than vendor claims or feature checklists. It also produced a clearer picture of where each tool fit the work: what tasks people brought to each platform, what friction points stopped usage, and what drove switching behavior.

Reflection

AI adoption scales when the measurement layer is real, stable, and repeatable—so tool choices and internal builds are driven by observed work patterns, not mandate.

Across all three moves, the through-line was reducing ambiguity. Adoption improves when the first experience is useful, when demand is captured explicitly, and when leadership decisions are backed by measured user behavior. That is how you turn AI uptake from pockets of expertise into a company capability.
