Product Signals for Scientific Tools

decision systems · organization · high

Installing product discipline into a research organization where tools were specced in Slack and launched without adoption plans, using lightweight PRDs, project posters, and adoption tracking to accelerate shipping and clarify tradeoffs.

Context

Internal discovery tools decide which experiments get prioritized, yet they were being specced in Slack and launched without adoption plans. Installing product discipline meant scientists stopped guessing whether a tool worked and leadership could see the tradeoff becomes obvious instead of theoretical when two platforms competed for the same quarter.

The tooling backlog spanned similarity search, compound analytics, cross-reference dashboards, and data reporting. None had requirements, personas, or success metrics. Whoever yelled loudest got engineering cycles, and the only governance was “ship before the OKR checkpoint.”

Ownership

I owned product framing personally—writing the documents, defining cadences, and proving that a little structure let us ship faster and with clearer accountability. I worked directly with the platform engineering lead to redefine product vs. engineering responsibilities and with medicinal chemistry, biology, and modeling teams to shape adoption strategy. Leadership visibility was mine to architect.

Decision Frame

The core problem: data work loses leverage when the same chemist has to both define and implement a feature with no cross-functional visibility. Without adoption tracking we could not tell whether a tool reduced a 48-hour manual analoging process to minutes or just added another interface to maintain.

Scientists distrusted anything that looked like bureaucracy, so any framework I introduced had to feel like an accelerant, not overhead. The decision I took was to layer product management practices into a company that had never seen a PRD—without creating the perception of overhead.

My approach was tiered by impact and uncertainty:

High-impact infrastructure work (similarity search) got full PRDs: 22 requirements across four phased deliveries, explicit success metrics (reduce analoging from 1–2 days to <60 seconds), dependencies, and non-goals. That document forced engineering to select the right chemistry toolkit and leadership to see why we needed a multi-phase rollout rather than a heroic push.

Medium-scope analytics surfaces (compound analytics, data reporting) got one-page project posters: problem statement, user personas, success signal, launch plan, and adoption checkpoints. The compound analytics app moved from concept to production inside one quarter because everyone could see the milestone path.

Incremental fixes (cross-reference operations) lived in a rolling bug ledger.

I coupled posters with adoption segmentation. Instead of declaring victory at launch, we tracked uptake by role (medicinal chemistry, biology, modeling) and by use case (nomination prep, executive reporting). That exposed, for example, that the data reporting interface landed well with modeling but lagged in biology due to training gaps.

I rewrote our OKRs so objectives described the outcome (“Reduce time to data reporting and analoging”) and key results split into product milestones (PRD approved, adoption thresholds met) and engineering milestones (infrastructure delivered, latency targets hit). In a context where domain experts wore every hat, naming the split gave us permission to push back when requirements arrived as half-formed Slack messages. It also protected engineers from being the default decision-makers.

I built a UX Research workspace: lightweight personas, interview notes, and a log of pain points. Writing it down avoided relearning the same lessons each quarter. Finally, I made launch plans include production health. The cross-reference tool had suffered from six-week data sync delays affecting 46 protocols, so I formalized a production-readiness checklist: monitoring, owner, data dependencies, rollback plan. Product ownership now extended past “ship it” to “keep it working.”

Outcome

Tools started landing with intent. The compound analytics app shipped inside a single OKR cycle because the poster aligned medicinal chemistry, biology, and engineering on scope and timing. The similarity search PRD locked infrastructure choices early, so we stopped bikeshedding technology stacks and focused on the 22 requirements. That tool is on track to compress a 1–2 day workflow to under a minute, and everyone can see the measurement plan inside the PRD rather than treating it as folklore.

Adoption dashboards told us exactly which departments had embraced the data reporting interface and which needed intervention; training became targeted, not generic. When the cross-reference platform failed, we had a bug ledger and production owner to run the incident, leading to rapid fixes for the pipeline gap and the sync delay.

OKR reviews changed tone. Instead of arguing whether a feature was “done,” we reported on product signals: PRD approved, milestone shipped, adoption threshold hit. Engineering used that same structure to push back on mid-cycle feature asks, citing the sequenced backlog. Leadership could now decide to slip or accelerate work with eyes open because the documentation spelled out effort and impact.

The UX research space lowered the barrier to stakeholder conversations; new engineers could skim personas and learn context without hour-long meetings. Most importantly, the platform team viewed our requests as credible because they arrived with clear requirements, definition of done, and adoption targets. That credibility helped keep the shared engineering queue moving even when priorities shifted mid-quarter.

Reflection

A principled product practice inside a science org is a leverage play: a few pages of structure keep tools aligned with users, expose trade-offs for leadership, and free engineers to focus on build quality instead of decoding hallway requests.

The key insight was meeting scientists where they were—not imposing “enterprise” process, but showing that lightweight documentation (posters, adoption dashboards, production checklists) actually accelerated shipping by removing ambiguity. The tiered approach (full PRD for foundational work, one-pagers for tactical, bug ledger for incremental) ensured the burden scaled with stakes, not dogmatically.
