Operating Discipline in Compound Nominations

decision systems · organization · high

Building a structured, auditable nomination system that turned ad-hoc target selection into an operating rhythm, improving hit rate enrichment and decision traceability across three years and five-plus biological targets.

Context

Each nomination wave locked $30K–$100K of procurement and months of wet-lab capacity. A sloppy compound picklist translated directly into sunk experimental runs with no way to learn from the outcome.

The company’s computational drug discovery group could produce ranked compound lists on demand, yet every screening cycle restarted from zero. Scientists ran their own filters, mixed vendor catalogs with local enumerations, and argued from memory because knowledge lived in a system or in someone’s head. Assay readouts landed six months later with no mechanism to attribute success or failure back to the initial decision. The consequence: we could not learn whether structural diversity constraints mattered, whether AI rankings were overfitting, or whether procurement dollars were allocated to the right exploration vs. exploitation balance.

The stakes were high. Compounds that shipped through procurement became fixed experimental commitments. A process that felt fast but erased traceability meant every nomination became an isolated decision event rather than a learning loop.

Ownership

I owned the decision of whether to institutionalize the nomination process—imposing structure across data science, modeling, medicinal chemistry, and biology—or tolerate ad-hoc pulls that felt fast but prevented learning. The core problem was socio-technical, not algorithmic: integrating ML prioritization, chemist heuristics, vendor constraints, and finance guardrails into one decision that could be audited later.

I designed, built, and maintained the six-stage system and decision rituals. I worked closely with medicinal chemists to encode their filtering logic, partnered with leaders on scenario design and assumption-setting, and coordinated with procurement and vendor teams to close the final mile into purchase orders.

Decision Frame

The Problem: Each target nomination restart meant rebuilding data context. No central record of why a compound made the cut. No way to replay a decision months later when assay data arrived. No feedback loop to improve future nominations.

The Options:

  • Keep ad-hoc: low friction, high speed, zero traceability, no learning
  • Institutionalize: higher friction upfront, but auditable decisions, repeatable process, measurable outcomes

The Rationale: Option B. Each nomination cycle locked millions in execution cost. The marginal cost of structure—standardized briefs, centralized data, parametric filters, cross-functional forums—was tiny compared to the value of learning whether compounds succeeded or failed and why. The structure also enabled a new analytical capability: task-correlation analysis to detect when supplemental screening would generate independent signal vs. just replicate prior data.

Constraints: Speed still mattered. Process could not create bottlenecks. I engineered for reuse—parameter sets versioned, new targets brought online by copying templates and editing counts, not rebuilding dashboards.

Outcome

The result was an operating system for nominations that survived three years across five-plus biological targets without rewriting process docs, and each run finished inside its procurement window.

Key results:

  • AI-powered enrichment: One AI-prioritized campaign delivered ~33× enrichment—9% hit rate against a 0.27% unranked baseline—because the ranking stayed intact through procurement instead of being re-filtered manually.

  • Decision clarity: Leadership reviews stopped being binary “yes/no” votes and became conversations about cost, diversity, and learning scenarios with explicit sensitivity tables showing cost-of-learning tradeoffs.

  • Portfolio optimization: Task-correlation analysis prevented waste. Example: declined a supplemental run whose compounds overlapped 82% with existing assays; greenlit another with orthogonal coverage (under 15% overlap). That analytical layer reframed nominations in portfolio terms instead of per-target hunches.

  • Escalation structure: When orthogonal assay data threatened a near-zero hit-to-lead rate, the decision record let me escalate with structured options (pause procurement, adjust filters, commission a parallel assay line) instead of generic panic.

  • Operational efficiency: Procurement automation shaved days off lead times and prevented “lost” compounds because every approved list flowed straight into vendor APIs with audit IDs. Onboarding new scientists became trivial—the entire philosophy and trade-offs lived in the system instead of scattered across Slack threads.

The six-stage architecture:

  1. Target briefs — Templated, structured schema capturing compound counts, diversity guardrails, legal procurement limits, assay timing, bespoke chemistry requirements, and parameter diffs between targets.

  2. Centralized model outputs — Internal registry with metadata on training set, inference date, ranking logic. No CSV emails. One source of truth feeding the pipeline.

  3. Structural/property filters — Parameterized Lipinski constraints, vendor availability, scaffold diversity, domain-specific blocklists. Chemists toggled filters with visible tradeoffs (more money on procurement vs. more time cleaning lists).

  4. Nomination packages — Interactive dashboards showing four scenarios: $30K curated, $30K exploratory, $60K curated, $60K exploratory. Each surfaced hit-rate assumptions, model uncertainty bands, property distributions, procurement lead times, and documented assumptions inline so decisions could be replayed months later.

  5. Decision forums — Cross-functional leads walked through scenarios, challenged assumptions, committed to a selection with written rationale. Produced an auditable log revisited when assay data arrived.

  6. Procurement handoffs — Automated through vendor quoting API so approved list translated directly into purchase orders, removing another source of slippage.

Reflection

Building data work that survives beyond a single experiment means treating decision flow as the product. This project proved that disciplined structure beats heroic guessing when compound nominations decide the next year of discovery work.

What I’d do differently: The analytical layer—task-correlation analysis, hit-rate enrichment metrics, diversity spread calculations—came late. I should have embedded the measurement system from the start instead of bolting it on after two nomination cycles.

Lessons: Narrating leverage explicitly matters. When ML rankings conflicted with medicinal chemistry preferences, I translated disagreement into cost-of-learning terms: maintain a larger screening footprint now with higher reagent spend, or tighten filters early and accept higher epistemic risk. I picked the larger footprint during first-in-target campaigns because marginal value of information dwarfed marginal cost of compounds. Documenting that logic meant the default could shift later once empirical data justified narrower passes.

Future implications: The structured retrospective ritual—pairing procurement records, assay outcomes, and rationale logs—generated concrete deltas such as “hydrophobicity cap removed 11% of eventual hits” or “vendor substitution delays averaged nine days.” Those metrics fed directly into the next round’s parameters. Institutionalizing that feedback loop across three years proved the concept: decision discipline compounds.
