Data Quality Is an Incident Discipline

decision systems · organization · high

Building incident response protocols and lineage-aware monitoring transformed silent data failures into defensible decisions in a drug discovery pipeline.

On this page

  • Context
  • Ownership
  • Decision Frame
  • Outcome
  • Reflection

Context

A single misspelled configuration field quietly pushed six FDA-approved compounds through three discovery programs and wasted about $90, but the real damage was the doubt it sowed in a pipeline that controls six-figure purchasing decisions. The production data platform powering dashboards, nomination tools, and leadership reviews ingested external sources, applied layered transformations, and surfaced boolean flags that dictated procurement. The underlying problem: data quality failures in applied AI workflows rarely explode loudly. They produce plausible numbers that lie quietly until a scientist notices a weird compound on a list months later. Without structured incident response, those errors erode trust faster than they burn dollars.

Ownership

I diagnosed and owned the incident response protocol design. The FDA flag was mine to trace from the nomination tool back through four transformation layers to the seed file controlling dynamic property pivots—and mine to escalate as a governance gap, not “just” a code bug. I codified the incident pattern into a playbook, layered proactive controls (configuration tests, lineage monitors, incident registry), and paired technical safeguards with social communication (stakeholder briefs, escalation paths). When procedural gaps emerged—teams omitting failure records—I escalated to leadership with evidence and pushed enforcement. The team implemented the playbook; I owned its design and the case for why it mattered.

Decision Frame

The decision: Treat the FDA incident as a one-off—fix the typo and move on—or turn it into proof that we needed lineage-aware monitoring, non-generic tests, and structured incident response protocols.

The problem statement: One row had a plural instead of a singular. The pivot generated a boolean column that evaluated to FALSE for every compound, including FDA-approved drugs that should have been filtered out. Six compounds across three programs slipped through before a colleague noticed. Fixing the typo was necessary but insufficient. The real risk: silent propagation across four transformation layers without alerting anyone, and no way to prevent recurrence.

The approach: Frame the blast radius (three programs, six compounds, ~$90 wasted spend) and escalate the incident as a governance gap. Then codify an incident pattern: diagnose (follow data upstream until you reach the first wrong assumption), quantify (express impact in rows, dollars, programs, and decisions so leadership sees the business cost), fix (correct the issue and verify downstream propagation), test (build a non-generic assertion that would have caught this exact failure), and brief (tell every affected stakeholder what happened, what changed, and how recurrence is prevented). Write it as a playbook so anyone on the team could run it.

Constraints: The measurement layer had to remain real, stable, and repeatable before the next compound list went out. Teams had to trust the output again. The solution had to scale beyond this one incident to catch the next five.

Outcome

Trust recovered because people saw the system change. Scientists received an incident brief within 24 hours of the FDA issue, along with screenshots of the corrected data. The new configuration tests immediately caught two additional typos before they reached production. Lineage monitors flagged a stalled ingestion job before it aged past a day, preventing a repeat of the 15-day staleness incident. The compliance escalation led to weekly audits of fail records and a dashboard tile that shows completeness by program, so leadership can question metrics when completeness drops.

The incident registry gave us trend data; by quarter two the rate of severity-two incidents dropped because we were catching issues at the seed layer instead of at the dashboard. When a new teammate joined, they could read the playbook and run an incident themselves, which meant data quality was no longer personality-driven. Most telling, nomination reviews stopped opening with “can we trust this?” and returned to debating actual compound trade-offs.

Over twelve months, the registry covered five major incidents: the FDA boolean typo, a crawler staleness issue that left a metrics dashboard 15 days behind, a compliance gap where research teams skipped fail records, a schema drift in an external vendor feed, and a data contract breach that truncated assay results. Each incident followed the same loop and produced new safeguards.

Reflection

Data quality in drug discovery isn’t about perfect code; it’s about owning incident response so silent errors surface fast, decisions remain defensible, and scientists can spend their time on leverage instead of forensic spreadsheet work. The tradeoff became obvious instead of theoretical: spend a little time entering fail data now or keep making portfolio decisions on biased funnels. Because data quality is as much social as technical, the communication layer mattered as much as the monitoring layer—and escalation paths forced the organization to choose where it stood on quality vs. speed.
