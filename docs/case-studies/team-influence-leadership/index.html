Influence Comes From Decision Infrastructure

execution leadership · organization · high

How reframing a reactive data team as a strategic decision intelligence function transformed organizational impact through governance, named research portfolios, and quantitative prioritization frameworks.

On this page

  • Context
  • Ownership
  • Decision Frame
  • Outcome
  • Reflection

Context

I took a reactive data work team and made it a strategic partner in drug discovery investment decisions over 18 months. The cost of staying in “service desk” mode was decision-integrity risk, burnout, and missed portfolio leverage: we were informing choices worth tens of thousands of dollars per round plus months of downstream lab work without any seat at the table.

The inherited operating model was simple: stakeholders sent ad-hoc requests (“pull hit rates for this target,” “how many compounds passed the filter?”) and we delivered fast, accurate outputs. That responsiveness created three compounding failure modes.

First, analyses traveled without their assumptions. Numbers built for one question landed in dashboards and decks for other questions, stripped of provenance and caveats. The result was not “bad data”; it was context loss, which is how decision-makers end up overconfident.

Second, the team had no strategic voice. We were asked to confirm narratives after they were already formed, not to shape the framing upstream. The work produced decision inputs, but the team was treated as a delivery mechanism.

Third, reactive throughput blocked compounding learning. Every request was urgent, none accumulated into a durable research agenda, and senior analysts repeated the same patterns six months apart. Knowledge lives in a system or in someone’s head, and in this setup it lived in people—until they got tired.

Ownership

I owned the operating model redesign end-to-end: governance design, the research portfolio structure, the strategic charter, and the quantitative prioritization framework. I moved the team upstream by converting its work from service delivery into strategic research, anchored on measurability and decision support.

Decision Frame

The decision in front of me was whether to optimize the service desk (more throughput, better templates) or change the system so the team’s work attached to the decisions it was meant to inform. I chose to build decision infrastructure: governance to preserve context, a named research portfolio to make work legible, and a charter plus prioritization framework to move the team upstream.

I ran a three-move sequence that traded short-term responsiveness for long-term leverage, with the known tradeoff of introducing friction into ad-hoc requests while the new system took hold.

Move One: Data Governance Protocol

The immediate risk was analyses circulating without context, so I escalated it as decision-integrity exposure, not process hygiene. The fix was concrete: all ad-hoc data requests funneled through a managed intake before distribution. This was not gatekeeping; it was attaching assumptions to outputs every time. Intake meant every analysis shipped with scope limitations, caveats, and intended use, so downstream reuse stayed honest. The tradeoff was obvious instead of theoretical: stakeholders lost a little speed, and the organization gained repeatable interpretation.

Move Two: Named Research Agenda

The team was already doing real investigative work inside ad-hoc tickets—promiscuity analysis, undesirable compound flagging, novel collection characterization—but it had no identity. I turned that buried work into a portfolio called “Waypoints.” Each Waypoint was a self-contained investigation with a defined question, a method, and a deliverable. We tracked Waypoints as strategic learning, not service backlog. This made work visible and comparable. It also changed how the team spoke: “I ran numbers” became “we completed the promiscuity Waypoint and found that 40% of our library has non-selective activity profiles.” The tradeoff was naming and scoping overhead; the upside was that leadership could see an analytical program with accumulating insights instead of isolated outputs.

Move Three: Strategic Charter and Quantitative Framework

I built a strategic charter that defined the team as a decision intelligence and scientific enablement function, anchored on four pillars:

  1. Portfolio strategy and data-driven prioritization — using data to shape which programs to invest in, not only which compounds to nominate
  2. Knowledge systems — building the data warehouse, governed datasets, and documentation that makes institutional knowledge durable and queryable
  3. Experimental simulation — computational approaches that reduce wet-lab load or improve experimental design
  4. Decision intelligence — dashboards, frameworks, and reporting that make investment decisions traceable and evidence-based

The charter included proposed KPIs (target selection efficiency, decision velocity, portfolio success rate, operational efficiency) and went to the VP and CTO as a definition document: scope, measurement, organizational fit. The known tradeoff was committing to measurability—once you propose KPIs, you can be held to them. That constraint was the point.

To give the charter teeth, I built a portfolio prioritization framework called CUE (Criticality, Upside, Evidence). CUE is a probability-of-success model that scores each active project across three dimensions on a 1–5 scale: Criticality (what’s at stake), Upside (impact if it hits), Evidence (strength of the current base). Those scores feed a concave power function that maps to a probability-of-success uplift between a defined floor and ceiling. I chose concavity because it encodes the real economics of early evidence: going from “no evidence” to “some evidence” matters more than going from “strong” to “very strong,” and marginal gains diminish as you approach the ceiling.

I grounded the parameters in published pharma benchmarks so the model reflected drug discovery reality rather than arbitrary scaling. AstraZeneca’s 5R work showed that applying structured criteria to early-stage decisions improved lead-to-candidate success rates from roughly 23% to 48%. I anchored the floor/ceiling around that result and related SpringerLink citations. CUE then became a shared language for “incommensurable” comparisons: strong biology with weak chemical matter versus deep chemistry with uncertain biology. It made the tradeoffs explicit and quantitative, and it gave leadership a tool to discuss portfolio bets with evidence as the center.

Outcome

The governance protocol became standard operating practice: ad-hoc requests routed through managed intake, with distribution controlled and tracked on a dedicated dashboard so context traveled with outputs. Waypoints became formalized under the team’s OKR structure, turning invisible analytical effort into a visible research program with named deliverables and reusable methods. The strategic charter reframed the team’s scope and positioned it as “decision intelligence and scientific enablement,” language that stuck in leadership conversations. CUE was presented to the CEO as a benchmark-grounded tool for portfolio-level prioritization, enabling quantitative discussions across projects that previously relied on anecdote and rhetorical strength. As a result, the team’s work expanded beyond data services into product management, strategic analysis, and decision support—without adding grinding hours, by converting repeated analysis into durable systems.

Reflection

Team influence scales when the measurement layer is real, stable, and repeatable, and when your data work is engineered to preserve context and shape decisions rather than simply answer requests. The shift from service desk to strategic partner was not about working harder; it was about building systems that made the team’s thinking legible and attached to decisions that mattered. Accountability through KPIs was the lock that made the strategic frame stick—once the team was measured on decision velocity and portfolio success, not throughput, the entire operating model inverted.
