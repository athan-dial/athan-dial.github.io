9. ‚ùå **Passive voice excuses** - "mistakes were made" ‚Üí "we made a mistake"

16. ‚ùå **Vague recommendations** - "Work smarter, not harder"

## 7 Sentence Structure Templates

### 1. Opening with Clear Declaration + Detail
**Pattern**: `<Main outcome>, with <detail>`

**Example**: "This week the group made measurable progress across four workstreams, with MARS and the Learning Agenda delivering the most forward movement."

**Use when**: Starting status updates or summaries

### 2. Compound Parallel Clauses
**Pattern**: `<Action A continues to do X>, while <Action B did Y>`

**Example**: "Cost-projection work for XtalPi continues to shape our 2026 chemical space strategy, while CIDER user research surfaced several meaningful UX insights from on-site sessions that will shape upcoming roadmap adjustments."

**Use when**: Reporting concurrent activities

### 3. Em-dash for Definition/Amplification
**Pattern**: `<Statement> ‚Äî <defining clause>`

**Example**: "We're excited to announce Montai's comprehensive ChatGPT Training & Enablement initiative ‚Äî a company-wide program designed to empower every team member with the skills, tools, and confidence to harness AI for real productivity gains."

**Use when**: Adding clarification without breaking flow

### 4. Colon-Introduced List
**Pattern**: `<Lead-in statement>: <item 1>, <item 2>, <item 3>, and <item 4>`

**Example**: "Key principles: progressive training cadence, measurable ROI, a growing champion network, and continuous iterative improvement."

**Use when**: Enumerating multiple related items inline

### 5. Rhetorical Question + Answer
**Pattern**: `<Question>: <answer>`

**Example**: "What 'uncorrelated' means in plain English: the three scaffolds are meaningfully different chemistries, not trivial variations."

**Use when**: Introducing potentially unfamiliar terms

### 6. Short Imperative for Emphasis
**Pattern**: `<Brief command>. <Explanatory sentence with dash for qualification>`

**Example**: "Take time to learn. Don't get sucked into the minutia of decision-making too early ‚Äì there will be plenty of time for that later."

**Use when**: Giving direct guidance or recommendations

### 7. Conditional If/Then Logic
**Pattern**: `If <condition>, we <action>: <specific steps>`

**Example**: "If the number is light, we act: rebalance orders, fast-track under-represented scaffolds, add small analog top-ups, or swap vendors."

**Use when**: Outlining decision criteria and responses

## Context-Specific Tone Calibrations

### Slack (Concise & Friendly)
- **Greeting**: "Hi all," "@here quick update:" ":wave: Welcome..."
- **Emoji use**: Sparingly - üëç üöÄ ‚úÖ üòä for tone
- **Structure**: Bullets with line breaks, even in chat
- **Tone markers**: Occasional exclamation (single, not multiple)
- **Acknowledgment**: Quick thanks, specific praise
- **Example**: "Thanks for the update ‚Äì this is great progress."

### Email (Polite & Structured)
- **Opening**: "Hi [Name]," or "Hello team,"
- **Purpose line**: State email purpose upfront
- **Structure**: Paragraphs by topic or bullets
- **Contractions**: Fine ("we're", "I'll", "you'd")
- **Closing**: "Thanks," or "Best," + name
- **Tone**: Professional but approachable

### Documentation (Comprehensive & Explanatory)
- **Voice**: Third person or first-person plural
- **Structure**: Clear headings, bullets, tables
- **Formatting**: Bold for key terms, italics for definitions
- **Completeness**: Provide context so doc stands alone
- **Links**: Liberal use of hyperlinks for references
- **Tone**: Factual and instructional

### Presentations (Focused & Narrative)
- **Slides**: Minimal text, clear visuals
- **Headlines**: Questions or declarative statements
- **Flow**: Introduction ‚Üí key points ‚Üí evidence ‚Üí conclusion
- **Speaking**: Elaborate beyond what's on slide
- **Data**: Always explain what charts/numbers mean
- **Backup**: Appendices for detail

### Stakeholder Updates (Matter-of-fact & Balanced)
- **Structure**: Executive summary ‚Üí wins ‚Üí risks ‚Üí next steps
- **Lead**: Most important information first
- **Language**: High-level descriptors, avoid deep jargon
- **Tone**: Respectful of time, focused on outcomes
- **Risks**: Candid but always note mitigation
- **Closing**: Clear ask or next step if needed

## Summary: Core Voice Principles

1. **Precision over vagueness** - Specific terms, concrete examples
2. **Confidence over hedging** - "Will" not "might", facts not guesses
3. **Evidence over assertion** - Data first, conclusions second
4. **Structure over stream-of-consciousness** - Bullets, sections, flow
5. **Reader-oriented** - Anticipate questions, provide context
6. **Professional yet approachable** - Warm but not casual
7. **Balanced** - Acknowledge trade-offs and limitations
8. **Action-oriented** - Every sentence serves a purpose
